The Curious Case of Duality

A journey into the quantum realm through the lives of Noble Prize winning father, and son; physicists, JJ Thomson and his son George Thomson.

On December 18, 1856 a genius was born that would raise a son that would mirror his father. Joseph John Thomson was that father. Through the help of his experiments the verification of electrons was made and atomic models followed. His son, George Paget Thomson would later contribute greatly to his father’s works showing that the particle that his father had discovered also had properties that mimic waves. Although not Chemist themselves, their contributions led to both of them receiving Nobel Prizes in their pioneering research in helping to describe an electron that is very useful in the field of Chemistry (NobelPrize).

Joseph John Thomson’s experiment which led to some conclusions that his son would later perfect involved cathode ray tubes. This experiment would show that all atoms have a very tiny negatively charged subatomic particles that are called elections (KHAN).

The experiment worked by sealing glass tubes and having all the air vacuumed out. At either end of the tubes are electrodes. When a high voltage is applied to these electrodes a beam of particles flow from the cathode to the anode. The cathode is negatively charged electrode and the anode is positively charged anode. This device is called a Cathode Ray for the simple fact that the particle beam starts at the cathode. A material known as phosphors will emit a light, spark, as it detects a ray that has “paint[ed]” it (KAHN).

Thomson being the genius he was placed two oppositely charged electric plates on opposite sides of the cathode. The ray that was emitted by the cathode ray pushed away from the negatively charged electric plate, and pulled towards the positively charged plate. Thomson concluded that the cathode ray itself must be electrically charged (KAHN).

The experiments would lead to major discoveries that helped to describe characteristics of the particle. Thomson discovered the mass to charge ratio of the particles emitted by the cathode ray. This meant that this particle was really really small. So small, it was smaller than any atom that was known at the time (KAHN).

Thomson was thorough, he did the experiment over and over with different electrode materials, but the same results persisted (KAHN). He concluded that these particles were negatively charged, that they existed as part of an atom, and that they could be found in all elements (KAHN).

Sir Joseph J Thomson, Mathematical Physicist, President of the Royal Society 1916-1920, commenting on Electrical Engineering in his own words on film, October 18th 1934, he was 78 at the time. He emphasized, “that all major discovers were made without any practical thought of application”. And, that some discovers “seemed quite unfitting for such a purpose… Consider the discovery of the electron. Could anything at first sight seem so impractical”. He then quotes Shakespeare in the most amazing way, “My use is great, because I am so small”.

Joseph John Thomson spent years lecturing on physics, and electrical engineering. In his book Recollections and Reflections on page 82, he writes: “Though eighteen hours a week spent in teaching may be thought to leave little time for research, … There is no better way of getting a good grasp of your subject, or one more likely to start more ideas for research, than teaching it or lecturing about it, especially if you hearers know very little about it, and it is all to the good if they are rather stupid” (Institute of Electrical Engineering).

Teaching was his passion, a rhythmic experience to get closer to a subatomic truth. It was this passion that must had led him to be such a great role model of a father to raise such an excellent son.

His son is George Paget Thomson, born 1892 at Cambridge. In a testimony to the father’s teachings, he grew up to attend Cambridge, followed his father’s footsteps in physics, and even conducted research with his father when in 1914, war began. George doing what was right, joined the Queen’s Regiment of Infantry. Later he would help the war effort in other ways, including “aerodynamical problems” (NobelPrize).

When the war ended he became a lecturer and conducted research in the field of physics at Corpus Christi College, Cambridge. It was later when teaching as a Professor at University of Aberdeen that he would conduct experiments that would forever change the way scholars would look upon the electron that his father had help to discover (NobelPrize).

The experiment involved the very same electrons that his father once described, and having them go through a very thin piece of metal. There was an understanding that an electron was a particle and if it is such a thing, it must behave as such. This is not what George uncovered. His experiments showed that the electron was not displaying the characteristics of a particle, but instead that of waves. Unlike particles which are packaged and quantized, while waves show patterns of diffraction. This opened the door for from Classical Mechanics to Quantum Mechanics (NoblePrize).

Like father, like son. Like particle, like wave, the duality of it all. George never stopped teaching. He would go to work at Imperial College, where his passion led him to nuclear physics. There he contributed by experimenting on fission of uranium by neutron. Fission was discovered in 1939. George being the veteran that he was, thought it could be used for military purposes to help end wars. For the nature of an infantryman is to kill the enemy to ensure his brother in arms come home to their love ones. He convinced British Air Ministry to acquire a ton of uranium oxide. Although his experiments did not yield the results that he wanted, the knowledge that he gained helped later when the British Government needed his expertise to acknowledge that a bomb could be made with such material. This information was passed to their American counterparts (NobelPrize).

George Paget Thomson, Physics: Year of Award 1937, Lecture: Discovery of the Electron at the 15th Lindau meeting, 1965, in his own recorded words stated that “the electron was named before it was discovered. Johnstone Stoney, an Irish Physicist, perhaps it is difficult for his country to accept a word that did not exist” coined the word. George went on the say that “The electron now for nearly 70 years has stood for the fusion of two quite distinct ideas, and a failure to realize this has led to some unnecessary controversy that had better have been avoided”.

These two men, Giants in the field of physics have helped to shape the world that we find ourselves living in. Sir Joseph Thomson said it best, “That any new discover contains the germs of a new industry. It is said that great electrical firm in America instruct the members of their research department to discover something. No matter what. And, then to lead to another department, the business of making money out of it. And, I think experience has shown that that department generally does…. Scientific discovers are very efficient means of creating employment” (Institute of Electrical Engineers).

The people that live today live on the shoulder of Giants. Giants that have paved the way for others to live a life in comfort, to have jobs in ever advancing fields, and most of all, to already studied, researched, and made advancements in a field so that others can push science further than ever before. These advancements can only take place when individuals work together. As perfectly illustrated when George Thomson helped his father with research before the war. It is also perfectly illustrated by our advanced technological environment and how far we came. There is an old saying, “If I want what you have, I must do the things you have done”.

Both Joseph and George never stopped teaching, lecturing, speaking on the subject. Engulfed by these ideas on a daily basis, they were able to interrupt what needed to be done to advance the field of physics in Electrical Engineering. And, for George in Nuclear Engineering. In this, they helped changed the world, and maybe, just maybe, many individuals can be inspired to do what they have done. To become a Giant in the field of science.
